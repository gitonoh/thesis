<irf_loc_asym_dec>
- Following a positive valuation shock, the Bottom 50\% initially exhibits inert behavior, with the behavioral component remaining close to zero for a couple of quarters. This flat early response indicates that low‑wealth households do not actively rebalance during the initial phase of the boom and instead track the passive drift. Only after this period of inertia does the behavioral IRF begin to decline, reflecting delayed selling relative to the passive benchmark.
- The Next 40\% displays even greater inertia. Their behavioral response remains almost perfectly flat throughout the entire horizon, implying that mid‑wealth households make virtually no active adjustments in response to valuation increases. Their equity exposure simply follows the mechanical drift implied by market returns, with no detectable deviation from the passive path.
- In contrast, the Next 9\% and Top 1\% reduce their active exposure relatively early after the shock, producing an initial downward movement in the behavioral IRF. At horizons where the passive drift slows, however, the behavioral component rises modestly. This upward movement does not represent a pause but a partial catch‑up to the passive benchmark. In other words, wealthier households adjust their portfolios in a countercyclical manner: they sell early in the boom, then buy back some equity once the valuation shock dissipates and mechanical drift weakens. After this adjustment, the IRFs settle into a stable negative range, representing the persistent cumulative effect of earlier trades rather than continued rebalancing.
- Turning to negative valuation shocks, the Bottom 50\% displays an immediate and statistically significant decline in the active component, and the magnitude of this response is noticeably larger than their adjustment to positive shocks. This pronounced asymmetry reflects the heightened sensitivity of low‑wealth households to adverse valuation movements, consistent with precautionary motives and liquidity pressures that amplify their response when equity values fall. The Next 40\% shows responses that are largely indistinguishable from zero across horizons, indicating that mid‑wealth households do not meaningfully adjust their equity exposure even when valuations decline.
- Wealthier groups also reduce their active exposure following negative shocks, but their adjustments are smaller and unfold more gradually. The Next 9\% and Top 1\% exhibit early‑horizon responses that are close to zero before slowly drifting downward, suggesting that they are able to absorb valuation losses without immediate portfolio changes. Although they ultimately sell as well, the magnitude and timing of their responses differ markedly from those of the Bottom 50\%.

<irf_loc_asym_dec_flex>
- While the overall qualitative patterns remain consistent with the general‑benchmark results, several timing differences emerge. Following a positive valuation shock, the Bottom 50\% begins adjusting earlier than under the general benchmark, though the initial movement remains modest before the behavioral component gradually declines relative to the passive path. The Next 40\% also shows an earlier and clearer reduction in the active component, indicating that mid‑wealth households respond more visibly once the passive drift reflects their own market exposure.
- For the wealthier groups, the early‑horizon reduction in active exposure remains broadly similar under the flexible benchmark. The mid‑horizon upward movement likewise appears unchanged, reflecting the natural realignment of their portfolios when the group‑specific passive drift slows rather than indicating any shift in underlying behavior. As before, the behavioral IRFs settle into a stable negative range at longer horizons, capturing the lasting cumulative effect of earlier rebalancing rather than continued adjustment.
- For negative shocks, the Bottom 50\% now shows an initial response that is statistically indistinguishable from zero, narrowing the difference between their reactions to positive and negative shocks. The Next 40\% displays a significant decline from the initial horizon, indicating a clearer adjustment when valuations fall. By contrast, the Next 9\% and Top 1\% again exhibit no statistically meaningful deviation from zero at short horizons, confirming that their early‑stage responses remain muted even under the flexible drift.
